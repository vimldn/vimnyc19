In 1943, a <b>rent freeze</b> was imposed on many apartments to stabilize the rental market amid wartime pressures. This law created the <b>Rent Guidelines Board</b>, or RGB, to oversee annual rent adjustments. It applied mainly to buildings with six or more units built after 1947.</p> <p>In 1974, the Emergency Tenant Protection Act, or ETPA, expanded coverage to additional areas outside NYC and certain pre-1974 buildings. These laws together form the backbone of <b>rent control</b> and <b>rent stabilization</b>. Understanding this history helps clarify the key differences between rent control and rent stabilization in NYC.</p> <h2>Purpose and History</h2> <p>Rent control and rent stabilization protect tenants from excessive rent hikes amid NYC's low vacancy rate from the 2023 survey. These systems aim to ensure <b>affordable housing</b> in a tight rental market. They arose from historical housing shortages that drove up costs for renters.</p> <p>The timeline begins with <b>federal Emergency Rent Control</b> in 1943 during World War II. This imposed strict limits on rent increases to prevent profiteering amid wartime demand. It covered most NYC apartments built before 1947, setting the stage for ongoing rent regulation.</p> <p>In 1968, New York City introduced the <b>Rent Stabilization Law (RSL)</b> as a voluntary program. The key court case <i>Hotel Ass'n v. NYC</i> upheld its legality, allowing landlords to opt in for stabilized units. This marked a shift toward local control over <b>[<a href="/blog/how-do-i-know-if-my-nyc-apartment-is-rent-stabilized">rent stabilized</a>](/blog/how-do-i-know-if-my-nyc-apartment-is-rent-stabilized) leases</b>.</p> <p>By 1974, the state enacted the <b>Emergency Tenant Protection Act (ETPA)</b>, making stabilization mandatory for buildings with six or more units outside NYC but later applied citywide. This expanded protections to <b>pre-1974 buildings</b> and six-family buildings. Over decades, regulated units have declined due to vacancy decontrol and high rent vacancy deregulation.</p> <table><thead><tr><th>Year</th><th>Estimated Regulated Units in NYC</th></tr></thead> <tbody><tr><td>1970</td><td>~2 million</td></tr> <tr><td>2023</td><td>~1 million</td></tr></tbody></table> <p>The chart shows rent regulated apartments shrinking from about 2 million in 1970 to 1 million in 2023. It represents the strictest form of regulation from historical rent control laws started during World War II. These units survive mainly through <b>succession rights</b>, allowing family members to inherit tenancies.</p> <p>Rent control differs from rent stabilization by imposing hard caps on increases, not guidelines. Landlords must follow the Maximum Base Rent (MBR) system set by the Division of Housing and Community Renewal (DHCR). This keeps <b>controlled rents</b> far below market rates in eligible units.</p> <p>Only <b>first occupants</b> or their successors qualify, creating rare rent regulated apartments. Buildings with these units often feature original tenants who have lived there for decades. Tenants enjoy strong eviction protections, but the system phases out upon vacancy through vacancy decontrol.</p> <p>In practice, a tenant in a <i>pre-1947 Brooklyn brownstone</i> might pay $400 monthly while market rents top $2,500. This highlights <b>rent control law</b> as a legacy of New York City housing policy. It contrasts with the broader Rent Stabilization Law (RSL) covering more units.</p> <h3>Key Eligibility Criteria</h3> <p>Rent controlled apartments exist only in buildings with fewer than 6 units constructed before 1947. DHCR sets MBR biennially, with the last adjustment in 2017-18. This creates a <b>hard cap</b> unlike rent stabilization's guideline system from the NYC Rent Guidelines Board (RGB).</p> <ol><li>DHCR calculates and publishes MBR levels every two years.</li> <li>Landlord files MBR order application with DHCR, providing operating costs.</li> <li>DHCR approves typical increases of 7.5%-8% over base rent.</li> <li>Tenants have 90 days to challenge the order via administrative review.</li></ol> <p>Example: A $450 MBR base becomes $482 after the 2018 adjustment. Rent control is the older, stricter system applying to a small number of pre-1947 apartments, where rents are set directly by the government and tenants have lifetime rights. Rent stabilization, covering more units built before 1974, allows limited annual rent increases approved by a board, with tenants gaining protections upon vacancy but no perpetual rights.</p> <h3>How does rent control differ from rent stabilization in terms of coverage in NYC?</h3> <p>In NYC, rent control covers fewer than 2% of rental units, specifically those in buildings constructed before February 1947 with six or fewer units. Rent stabilization applies to about half a million units in buildings with six or more units built before 1974, making it far more widespread than rent control.</p> <h3>What are the rent increase rules under rent control vs. rent stabilization in NYC?</h3> <p>Under rent control in NYC, maximum rents are established by the NYC Rent Guidelines Board and adjusted annually based on costs, but increases require tenant consent or specific approvals. Rent stabilization allows annual increases voted on by the Rent Guidelines Board, typically 1-5%, applied to stabilized leases without the same strict caps as rent control.</p> <h3>Can tenants pass on protections to family under rent control and rent stabilization in NYC?</h3> <p>Rent control in NYC permits senior or disabled tenants to pass occupancy rights to immediate family members who lived with them for at least two years, providing lifelong succession. Rent stabilization allows succession to family members under similar conditions but only for one generation upon the tenant's death or permanent move, not as indefinitely as rent control.</p> <h3>What happens when a rent-controlled or rent-stabilized apartment becomes vacant in NYC?</h3> <p>In NYC rent control, a vacancy allows the landlord to renegotiate rent with the new tenant but it remains under control; stabilization sees a vacancy adjustment up to the full stabilized rent before applying guidelines increases, potentially leading to deregulation if rent exceeds certain thresholds post-2019 reforms.</p> <h3>Are rent control and rent stabilization affected differently by recent NYC housing reforms?</h3> <p>Recent reforms like the 2019 Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act impacted both in NYC, but rent stabilization saw major changes such as eliminating vacancy decontrol and capping preferential rents, while rent control's smaller pool retained its core strictures with minor adjustments to succession and harassment rules.</p>
